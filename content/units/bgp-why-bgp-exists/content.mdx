# Why BGP Exists

There is no "the internet" in the sense of a single network someone owns and operates. What we call the internet is tens of thousands of **separate** networks (run by ISPs, cloud providers, universities, banks, content companies) that have all agreed to exchange traffic. Each of those independently operated networks is an **Autonomous System (AS)**: one administrative domain, under one routing policy, with its own block of IP addresses.

The question this lesson answers is simple: **once you have all these independent networks, how does a packet find its way from one to another?** That is the job the Border Gateway Protocol (BGP) was invented to do.

## Inside one network: Interior Gateway Protocols

Within a single AS, routers need to learn how to reach each other. That's the job of an **Interior Gateway Protocol (IGP)**. You may already know the common ones:

- **OSPF** and **IS-IS**: link-state protocols that build a full map of the domain.
- **RIP**: an old distance-vector protocol (simple, limited).
- **EIGRP**: Cisco's advanced distance-vector protocol.

Different mechanics, but they share three assumptions:

1. **They optimize for the shortest path.** Lowest cost, fewest hops, fastest link: the goal is the mathematically best route to a destination.
2. **They assume mutual trust.** Every router in the domain is operated by the same organization, so routes are shared freely and believed without question.
3. **They're built for one administrative domain.** Tens, hundreds, maybe a few thousand routes: the scale of a single company's network.

Those assumptions are perfect *inside* an AS. They fall apart the moment you try to route the *entire* internet.

## Why IGPs can't run the internet

**Scale.** The global routing table, the "Default-Free Zone" (DFZ) table a backbone router must carry, currently holds **over 950,000 IPv4 routes**, and keeps growing. An IGP that floods a full map of every link would melt under that load. IGPs simply were never designed for hundreds of thousands of prefixes spanning tens of thousands of networks.

**Trust.** AT&T does not trust Google's routers, and shouldn't. Networks owned by different companies, sometimes competitors, cannot blindly believe whatever routes a neighbor sends. Routing between ASes needs to be *filtered* and *verified*, not freely shared.

**Policy.** This is the big one. Between networks, the "best" path is almost never the shortest one; it's the one that makes **business** sense. Consider these relationships:

- **Customer**: a network that *pays you* to carry its traffic. You happily route to and from it.
- **Peer**: a network you swap traffic with for free (settlement-free), but only your traffic and theirs.
- **Provider**: a network *you pay* to reach the rest of the internet.

A sane operator prefers sending traffic to a customer over a peer, and a peer over a (paid) provider, even when the paid path is shorter in hops. **An IGP has no way to express "this route is cheaper for my business."** It only knows distance. That gap is exactly what BGP fills.

## BGP: the one protocol between networks

BGP is the **Exterior Gateway Protocol (EGP)**: the protocol that runs *between* autonomous systems and glues them into the internet. In practice it is the *only* EGP in use; there is no competitor running the global table. Where IGPs handle the inside, BGP handles the borders between networks.

Its defining trait: **BGP prioritizes policy and control over shortest path.** It doesn't ask "what's the fewest hops?" It asks "given my business relationships, my filters, and what I'm willing to advertise, which path do I *choose*?" Reachability and policy drive the decision; hop count is far down the list of tie-breakers. BGP is less a navigation algorithm and more a system for **expressing and enforcing routing policy** at every network boundary.

## Built for scale and stability

Carrying ~950k+ routes across the planet demands a protocol that doesn't constantly redo its work. BGP is engineered for stability:

- **It runs over TCP** (port 179). TCP gives BGP reliable, ordered delivery, so BGP never has to build its own retransmission machinery.
- **It sends incremental updates, not periodic floods.** Once two routers exchange their full tables at session startup, they only send **changes**: a prefix appearing, a path withdrawn. There is no periodic re-flooding of the entire table the way older protocols re-announce everything on a timer.
- **That makes it quiet and stable at rest.** A link that doesn't change generates no traffic. With nearly a million prefixes, anything else would be unworkable.

## Who actually runs BGP?

Anyone who owns an **Autonomous System Number (ASN)** and connects to more than one other network:

- **ISPs and transit providers**: their whole business is moving traffic between networks.
- **Content and cloud providers** (Google, Cloudflare, Netflix, AWS): they announce their address space to the world and steer how users reach them.
- **Large or multi-homed enterprises**: a company connected to *two or more* upstream providers for redundancy runs BGP to use both links and survive losing one.

If you only have a single internet connection from one provider, you don't need BGP; a default route is enough. BGP earns its keep the moment you have **choices** to make at your network's edge.
